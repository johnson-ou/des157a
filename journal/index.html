Best Practices for Form Design

January 20, 2025

The article "Best Practices for Form Design" highlighted the significance of best practices in user forms and was surprising to me in a variety of ways. Although we use it so intuitively in our daily lives, I didn't realize the importance that forms serve as a crucial point of interaction between users and digital products. A key takeaway from the article is the importance of minimizing cognitive load for readers by simplifying forms. As designers, achieving this while keeping it intuitive such grouping related fields, using clear labels, and implementing progressive disclosure ensures users are not overwhelmed by too much information at once.

Another interesting point is the emphasis on error prevention and recovery. Real-time feedback to users, such as highlighting incomplete or incorrect fields as users fill out a form can prevent frustration and enhance task efficiency. Providing clear error messages instead of vague alerts ensures users understand what went wrong and how to fix it.

Additionally, the article emphasises a strong point on how to properly use placeholder text. Although the text in a placeholder can guide users to input correct data and improve form completion, user testing shows that placeholder texts often hurt usability rather than helping it. As users type in the field the hint disappears and if users forget the hint, they need to clear the text to see it again. An alternative is to place the hints outside of the input box, so users can refer back to it at any time.

A website form that exemplifies these best practices is Airtable's Form. Aggiework's recruitment form on Airtable demonstrates clarity through its simple layout, clear colored labels, and smooth validation process. The form is intuitive to navigate and accessible, providing a great user experience.
